    Robots Have Bodies but No Selves: The Persona Layer That Lets Them Become Human

    Humanoid robots are getting good at moving through the world and have no model of who they are or who they serve. Persona is the missing software layer of embodied AI, and it is what simulating humans has been building.

    Jintao Huang, Xiaomin Li, and Core Team
    July 1, 2026
    9 min read
    Position in brief

    Humanoid robots are improving fast at moving and manipulating the world, and they have almost no persona: no coherent identity of their own, and no working model of the people they share space with. We argue that persona is the missing software layer of embodied AI. It has two sides, a legible identity for the robot and a model of each human it serves, and it is the layer we have spent the last year building for simulated humans. The physical stack and the persona stack have grown on separate tracks, and they are about to need each other.

    Watch any recent humanoid demo and the progress is obvious in the body. The robot walks, balances, folds a shirt, pours a drink. Then it does the task for anyone, the same way, with no sense of whose kitchen it is in, what that person prefers, or who it is supposed to be. The hardware has a body. It has no self, and no model of you.

    1. The body is nearly solved; the "who" is empty

    Popular culture already imagines the version with a persona. In Quantic Dream's Detroit: Become Human, a world full of androids is compelling not because of the hardware but because each one is somebody:

    • Connor, a police investigator android: analytical, obedient, and methodical, built to hunt other androids and caught between his orders and his conscience.
    • Kara, a housekeeping android turned caretaker: gentle and protective, defined by the child she chooses to look after.
    • Markus, a caretaker android turned leader: the reluctant face of a movement, defined by the responsibility he takes on.

    Strip the personas out and you are left with identical machines standing in a room. Real robotics has the opposite problem. The bodies are arriving; the personas are not.

    Robotics has spent decades on perception, locomotion, and manipulation, and it shows. The layer that is missing sits above all of that: a stable identity and behavior policy for the robot, plus a representation of the humans around it. Today that layer is hardcoded or simply absent. A robot in a home is not a generic manipulator. It is a presence that a specific family has to live with, trust, and predict, and there is no software stack that gives it a consistent "who." Robotics feels this gap more than anyone; it is badly short on persona.

    This is not a call for personality as decoration. It is the observation that a machine which shares physical space with people has to make hundreds of small social decisions a minute, how close to stand, when to interrupt, whether to hand over the knife, and those decisions come from a model of self and others that the robot does not currently have.

    2. A robot needs persona in two directions

    The word "persona" points at two different objects, and an embodied agent needs both.

    01 / The robot itself

    Persona for the robot

    A coherent, inspectable identity that makes behavior legible and safe. How it talks, how cautious it is, what it will refuse, how it acts around a child versus an adult. Today this is scattered across ad hoc rules; it should be a structured, auditable representation, the same kind we build for simulated users.

    02 / The people it serves

    Persona of the human

    A model of a person's preferences, habits, and boundaries, so the robot anticipates instead of waiting to be told. This is the individual-simulation idea, the "representative," moved into a body. A home robot you must instruct from scratch every time is not useful; one that models you can act the way you would want.

    3. A robot's persona has to be inspectable, not vibes

    It is tempting to give a robot a personality with a paragraph of prompt and move on. That fails the moment the stakes are physical. A persona that drives a body needs the same discipline we ask of any high-stakes simulated agent: sources for what it believes about a person, explicit boundaries on what it may do, and confidence attached to every inference. When a robot decides that "this user is fine with me entering the bedroom," you need to know whether that came from an instruction, an observation, or a guess. The difference is the difference between helpful and dangerous.

    A robot that lives among people has to know who it is and who they are. That is a persona problem before it is a hardware one.

    4. You cannot A/B test a robot in someone's kitchen

    Here is why this is not cosmetic. Software products learn from live experiments: ship a variant, watch real users, keep what works. You cannot do that with a humanoid in someone's home. The cost of a bad interaction is physical, and the people in the room did not sign up to be the test set. High-stakes embodied behavior needs somewhere to fail safely before it meets a real person, and that somewhere is simulation.

    5. Rehearsal before reality: population simulation for robots

    This is exactly what our evaluation stack was built for. Before a behavior ships, you rehearse it against a simulated population: diverse personas, in a simulated home or workplace, reacting the way different real people would. You find where the robot frightens someone, misreads a request, or behaves unpredictably around an edge-case user, before any of it happens near a real person. Point that same machinery at a household and population simulation becomes a pre-deployment safety layer for embodied AI.

    6. From assistant to representative

    The second direction, the persona of the human, is where the everyday value lives. Imagine a robot that has learned, with explicit consent, how you like the kitchen organized, which interruptions you tolerate, and what you never want touched. It stops being a machine you operate and becomes a representative that acts as you would. Modeling one person's preferences and habits well enough to act on their behalf is the same capability a personal robot needs, now attached to hands. The promise comes with obligations: consent, editable memory, revocation, and hard limits on when the robot may act on a guess.

    7. Two modes, and knowing when to switch

    Here is a concrete guess at where this goes. A robot among people probably should not run at full model capability all the time. Most of the time it should be in a human mode: a bounded, consistent persona that is socially legible, predictable, and easy to be around. It moves, waits, and reacts like a specific person, not like an all-knowing oracle hovering in the kitchen.

    Then you ask it something that actually needs horsepower, how to fix the leak under the sink, what is wrong with this contract, and it switches into model mode: the full frontier system, brought to bear for as long as the task needs, and then handed back to the persona when the question is answered.

    This is where our two lines of work meet in one machine. Persona gives the robot a default self that people can actually live with. Capability control is the dial that decides how much raw model to expose, and when. The hard and interesting part is the switch itself: what triggers it, how the robot signals which mode it is in so nobody is surprised, and how it returns to being a predictable presence afterward instead of an oracle that never turns back off.

    8. What already transfers

    The reason this is close rather than speculative: persona for robotics is the problem we have been solving for software, with a body attached. Swap "user of an app" for "person in a room with a robot," and most of the machinery transfers.

    9. The hard parts

    Porting a persona into a body is not free, and the open questions are the interesting part of the agenda.

    • The embodiment gap. A simulated person reacting in text is not the same as a person reacting to a two-meter machine reaching past them. How much of a text-conditioned persona survives contact with physical presence is an empirical question.
    • Behavioral sim-to-real. Robotics already worries about transferring physical control from simulation to reality. Persona adds a second transfer problem: does behavior validated against simulated humans hold up with real ones?
    • Privacy and misuse. A robot that models a person accumulates an intimate profile, in the home, on a device with actuators. Data minimization, revocation, and action limits are not optional add-ons here; they are load-bearing.
    Persona for robotics.

    The next wave of embodied AI will not be won by better actuators alone. A robot among people needs a self and a model of the people around it, and that is software we can start building now, on top of what human simulation has already produced.

    10. Where this is going

    The physical stack and the persona stack have been growing on separate tracks. As robots move from demos into homes and workplaces, the missing layer stops being personality and starts being infrastructure: who the robot is, who it thinks you are, and how confident it should be about either. That is a simulation problem before it is a hardware one, and it is the one we think is about to matter most.
